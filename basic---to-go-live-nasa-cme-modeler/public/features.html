Spot The Aurora: a comprehensive public aurora forecast app

Spot The Aurora is a New Zealand-built aurora forecasting app designed to help ordinary people understand whether they might see the aurora, while also giving advanced users access to solar, heliospheric, solar-wind, IMF, magnetosphere, and visibility data in one place. Most people do not want raw space-weather numbers. They want to know: is it worth going outside? What direction should I look? Will I see it with my eyes, or only on camera? Is it getting better or worse? And what is actually causing the forecast to change?

NZ-specific aurora visibility forecast

New Zealand

Multi-horizon forecasts

The app provides aurora visibility forecasts at 15 minutes, 30 minutes, 1 hour, and 2 hours. The shorter forecasts use real-time solar wind and magnetometer data. The longer forecasts use current IMF trends and hemispheric power to estimate what may be coming.

New Zealand

Camera, phone, and eye visibility

Rather than just showing a number, the forecast tells you whether the aurora is likely to be camera-only, visible on a phone in night mode, or visible to the naked eye from your location. This is what most people actually want to know.

New Zealand

Darkness, moonlight, and local conditions

The score accounts for whether it is dark enough, whether the moon is up and bright, and whether twilight is interfering. A bright full moon above the horizon can wash out a faint aurora, so the forecast adjusts for this.

Aurora oval visualisation

Live aurora oval

The app displays a custom aurora oval that grows and shifts with forecast activity and substorm context. It is designed to make abstract space-weather values easier to understand by showing the approximate extent of the aurora in a visual format. The oval responds to live conditions and helps users gauge whether the aurora is likely to reach their latitude.

Solar activity monitoring

Solar

SUVI imagery and difference imagery

The app displays SUVI (Solar Ultraviolet Imager) imagery from GOES in multiple wavelengths. It also generates difference imagery, which highlights changes between frames and makes it easier to spot eruptions, coronal holes, and other activity that may produce space weather.

Solar

Sunspot and HMI imagery

A sunspot tracker shows active regions on the Sun with close-up imagery. HMI (Helioseismic and Magnetic Imager) data shows the magnetic structure of sunspot regions, which helps assess whether a region is likely to produce flares or eruptions.

Solar flare and CME tracking

Solar

Flare monitoring with CME association

The app monitors solar flares and, where possible, shows which flares have associated CME (coronal mass ejection) signatures. This helps users understand when solar activity may become Earth-directed space weather rather than just a flash on the Sun.

Coronagraphy and difference imagery

Solar

Multi-source coronagraph imagery

Coronagraph imagery from GOES CCOR, SOHO LASCO C2 and C3, and STEREO-A SECCHI COR2 is presented alongside app-generated difference imagery. Difference imagery is essential for identifying CME structure, direction, and speed as eruptions leave the Sun. Without it, faint CMEs can be invisible against the bright corona.

CME visualisation and modelling

Advanced

3D CME and high-speed stream visualisation

The CME visualisation page shows coronal mass ejections travelling through the heliosphere toward Earth. It includes official NOAA/SWPC forecast context where available, app-generated forecast estimates, and high-speed stream (HSS) wind structures from coronal holes. Individual CMEs can be modelled to help connect a specific eruption on the Sun to a possible arrival at Earth.

STEREO beacon J-plots

Advanced

Heliospheric imager J-map visualisation

STEREO-A HI1 and HI2 J-plot (time-elongation) style visualisations help follow outward-moving CME structures after they leave the coronagraph field of view. The app includes a field-of-view diagram showing where HI1 and HI2 are looking relative to the Sun and Earth. This is an interpretation aid, not an official forecast product.

Energetic particle monitoring (EPAM)

Experimental

Multi-spacecraft particle-rise detection

The app monitors EPAM-style energetic particle data from ACE, IMAP, SOLAR-1, and other available spacecraft. An algorithm looks for particle rises and compression signatures that may indicate incoming CME-related structures. Multiple spacecraft sources are used to reduce the chance of reacting to single-source data glitches. This is an experimental aid and not a guarantee of an incoming storm.

Solar wind and IMF monitoring

Solar wind

Live solar wind data

Real-time solar wind speed, density, temperature, and magnetic field strength from upstream L1 spacecraft. These values determine how much energy the solar wind is delivering to Earth's magnetosphere and are the primary driver of short-term aurora forecasts.

Solar wind

IMF Bz, By, Bt, and clock angle

The interplanetary magnetic field components are displayed alongside a clock angle dial showing the field direction in the By-Bz plane. Southward Bz is the single most important factor for aurora generation. The clock angle helps explain why aurora chances can change rapidly even when other numbers look similar.

Coupling and geomagnetic context

Solar wind

Newell coupling and dynamic pressure

The Newell coupling function combines solar wind speed, magnetic field strength, and clock angle into a single measure of how effectively the solar wind is driving the magnetosphere. Dynamic pressure shows the ram force of the solar wind against the magnetopause. Together they explain why speed plus magnetic orientation plus pressure can matter more than Kp alone.

Solar wind

Solar-wind phase context

The app attempts to classify the current solar wind into phases such as quiet, CME sheath, CME magnetic cloud, high-speed stream, or recovery. This context helps advanced users understand what kind of structure is passing Earth and what might come next.

Magnetotail and substorm potential

Experimental

Magnetotail loading and substorm context

A magnetotail visualisation estimates how loaded or stressed the tail may be based on sustained southward Bz and current conditions. When the tail is heavily loaded, a substorm release can produce a sudden aurora intensification. This feature provides context for whether conditions are building toward a substorm, but it is an estimate, not a certainty.

Russell-McPherron effect

Advanced

Seasonal coupling geometry

The Russell-McPherron effect describes how Earth's magnetic dipole tilt relative to the solar wind changes with the seasons, affecting how efficiently the IMF couples to the magnetosphere. The app visualises the current geometry and explains in plain language whether seasonal conditions are helping or limiting aurora generation. This is why spring and autumn equinoxes tend to produce more aurora activity.

NOAA 3-day Kp viewing outlook

New Zealand

Kp as a planning guide, not the whole forecast

The 3-day outlook uses NOAA/SWPC Kp forecasts mainly as a broad planning tool. The app layers Kp with darkness windows, moonrise and moonset times, and likely viewing conditions to show when a forecast Kp level might actually produce visible aurora from New Zealand. Kp alone is too broad for precise local visibility, which is why the live forecast uses real-time solar wind data instead.

Why Spot The Aurora is different

Many aurora apps focus mainly on Kp, auroral oval maps, basic alerts, and short-term visibility. Spot The Aurora goes further by combining solar source-region imagery, flare and CME tracking, coronagraph difference imagery, CME modelling, STEREO heliospheric imaging, EPAM particle monitoring, live solar wind and IMF data, coupling functions, substorm context, magnetotail visualisation, moonlight and darkness adjustment, and local visibility forecasts for New Zealand observers.

It is possibly the most comprehensive public-facing aurora forecast app built specifically for New Zealand and southern hemisphere observers. The goal is to cover the full chain from the Sun to your backyard, not just the last step.

Built for normal aurora watchers, with advanced data available

The app is designed to bridge expert-level space-weather data and normal people who simply want to know what they might see. The default forecast view answers the basic questions: is it worth going outside, what should I expect, and is it getting better or worse. For users who want to understand why, every layer of data is available: solar imagery, flare lists, coronagraph movies, CME tracks, solar wind charts, IMF components, coupling functions, and substorm context. You can use as little or as much as you want.

Limitations

Aurora forecasts are inherently uncertain. CMEs can arrive early, late, weak, strong, or miss Earth entirely. Bz can flip from north to south without warning. Substorms are difficult to predict in advance. Clouds and local weather still determine whether you can see anything regardless of what space weather is doing. Kp is a broad measure and not a precise local visibility forecast. Space-weather data can contain gaps, glitches, or temporary outages from source providers. The app should be used as guidance, not certainty.
