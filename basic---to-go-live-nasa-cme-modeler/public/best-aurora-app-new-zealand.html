What makes a good aurora app for New Zealand?

New Zealand is one of the best places in the inhabited world to see the Aurora Australis, but it sits at the edge of the auroral oval. That means aurora visibility depends on more factors than just "is there a storm happening." A good aurora app for NZ needs to account for all of them.

What NZ aurora watchers actually need

  • Southern hemisphere support. Many aurora apps are built for northern hemisphere observers. The aurora australis has the same physics but different geometry and different local conditions. An app should understand southern magnetic latitudes and southern viewing directions.
  • Local darkness and moonlight context. NZ's long summer twilight means aurora is only visible during winter and shoulder months when it gets properly dark. A bright moon can wash out a faint aurora. The app should factor in sunset, twilight, moonrise, moonset, and moon phase.
  • More than Kp. Kp is a 3-hour average that can lag real conditions by hours. A good app should use real-time solar wind and IMF data from upstream spacecraft to detect changes as they happen, not after the Kp index catches up.
  • Live Bz and IMF context. Southward Bz is what opens the door to aurora. Without monitoring Bz, clock angle, and coupling in real time, an app is flying blind on the single most important variable.
  • Substorm awareness. Aurora at NZ latitudes often arrives via substorms — sudden energy releases from the magnetotail. An app that does not account for substorm potential can miss the best viewing windows entirely.
  • Camera, phone, and eye visibility guidance. Most people want to know what they will actually see, not what the Kp number is. Will a phone pick it up? Will I see colour with my eyes? This kind of guidance makes the difference between a useful forecast and a confusing dashboard.
  • Clear explanations for beginners. Space weather is complex. A good app should make the forecast understandable for someone who has never heard of Bz, without dumbing down the data for people who have.
  • Advanced data for users who want to go deeper. Some users want to understand the full chain from the Sun to the aurora. Solar imagery, CME tracking, coronagraphy, solar wind data, coupling functions, and magnetotail context should be available for those who want it.

Where Spot The Aurora fits

Spot The Aurora was built in New Zealand for people who want a practical aurora forecast rather than a wall of unexplained space-weather numbers. It combines local NZ visibility guidance with advanced solar, CME, solar-wind, IMF, coupling, and substorm context.

The default view answers the basic questions: is it worth going outside, what visibility should I expect, and is the aurora improving or fading. For users who want to understand why, every layer is available: solar imagery, flare and CME tracking, coronagraph difference imagery, CME modelling, STEREO heliospheric imaging, EPAM particle monitoring, live solar wind charts, IMF clock angle, Newell coupling, dynamic pressure, magnetotail loading, Russell-McPherron seasonal context, and a 3-day Kp planning outlook with local darkness windows.

It is one of the most comprehensive public-facing aurora forecast apps available, and the most comprehensive built specifically for New Zealand and southern hemisphere observers. It is free to use.

A responsible note

No aurora app can guarantee visibility. Local weather, clouds, haze, light pollution, moonlight, and rapidly changing space-weather conditions all matter. Even a perfect forecast can be defeated by a cloud bank on the southern horizon. The best approach is to use the forecast as guidance, check the weather, find a dark spot with a clear southern view, and give your eyes time to adjust. The aurora rewards patience more than certainty.
