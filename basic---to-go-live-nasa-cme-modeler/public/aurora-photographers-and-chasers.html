An aurora app built for photographers and chasers

Spot The Aurora is built for everyday aurora watchers, but for photographers and chasers it becomes a full Sun-to-Earth decision tool. It combines practical New Zealand viewing guidance with advanced space-weather context, helping you decide when to go, what to expect, and why conditions are changing.

Why aurora photographers and chasers need more than Kp

Kp is useful as a broad planning guide, but it is not enough for real aurora chasing or photography decisions. It is a 3-hour average that can lag well behind what is actually happening. Whether you end up seeing aurora depends on live solar wind conditions, IMF orientation, Bz direction, coupling strength, substorm timing, how dark it is, how bright the moon is, where you are, cloud cover, and sometimes plain luck.

Spot The Aurora uses Kp mainly in the 3-day planning outlook. The live forecasts focus on more immediate data from upstream spacecraft and local magnetometers, because that is what actually tells you whether something is happening right now or about to happen in the next hour.

Camera, phone and eye visibility guidance

Spot The Aurora does not just say "aurora possible" and leave you guessing. It translates the forecast into practical visibility expectations so you know whether what is happening is likely to be camera-only, visible on a phone in night mode, or something you might see with your own eyes. This matters for photographers because a camera-only aurora still makes great photos, but you need to know you are not going to see structure or colour by eye before you drive somewhere.

Moonlight-aware aurora chasing

Aurora photography depends heavily on darkness. A bright moon above the horizon can wash out a faint aurora and reduce contrast in photos. Spot The Aurora adjusts its visibility expectations when the moon is bright and up, and it highlights darkness windows so you can plan around moonrise and moonset. If you have ever driven an hour to a dark sky site only to find the moon ruining your southern horizon, this feature is for you.

Short-term forecasts for real-world decisions

The app provides forecast windows at 15 minutes, 30 minutes, 1 hour, and 2 hours. The 15 and 30 minute forecasts use real-time solar wind data and are the most reliable. The 1 and 2 hour forecasts use IMF trends and hemispheric power to give you an idea of whether conditions are likely to improve, stay the same, or fade. These are meant to help you make real decisions: go now, wait a bit, keep watching, or call it a night.

Advanced space-weather context

For chasers and photographers who want to understand why the forecast is changing, the full data chain is available:

  • Solar wind speed, density and temperature
  • IMF Bz, By and Bt
  • IMF clock angle
  • Newell coupling function
  • Dynamic pressure
  • Russell-McPherron seasonal effect
  • Magnetotail loading and substorm context
  • EPAM particle-rise monitoring
  • CME and high-speed stream visualisation
  • Solar flare monitoring and CME association
  • Coronagraph imagery and difference imagery
  • STEREO HI1 and HI2 J-map style tools

You do not need to understand any of this to use the app. The simple forecast mode answers the basic questions on its own. But for the people who want to know exactly why Bz just flipped south at 2am and whether to get in the car, everything is there.

Responsible limitations

No aurora app can guarantee visibility. Space weather changes quickly. CMEs can arrive early, late, weak, strong, or miss Earth entirely. Solar wind data can contain gaps or glitches from source providers. Local cloud, haze, light pollution and terrain still matter. Spot The Aurora is a guidance tool, not a guarantee. The best approach is to use the forecast to decide when to try, find a dark spot with a clear southern view, and give your eyes time to adjust.

Frequently asked questions

What is the best aurora app for photographers in New Zealand?

Spot The Aurora is designed for New Zealand aurora photographers and chasers, combining short-term aurora visibility forecasts with solar wind, IMF, substorm, CME, moonlight, darkness, aurora oval and camera/phone/eye guidance.

Why is Kp not enough for aurora photography?

Kp is useful for broad planning, but aurora visibility can change quickly depending on live solar wind, Bz, IMF orientation, coupling, substorms, darkness, moonlight, cloud and location. The live forecast uses real-time data from upstream spacecraft instead.

Can Spot The Aurora tell me whether aurora will be visible by camera, phone or eye?

Yes. Spot The Aurora provides camera, phone and naked-eye visibility guidance to help users understand what they may realistically see under current and near-future conditions.

Does Spot The Aurora help with aurora chasing decisions?

Yes. The app is designed to help users decide whether to go out now, wait, keep watching, or prepare for changing conditions using short-term forecasts, solar-wind context, moonlight-aware scoring and advanced space-weather indicators.

Is visibility guaranteed?

No. Aurora forecasts are uncertain and local conditions such as cloud, haze, light pollution, terrain and rapidly changing space weather can affect what is actually visible.
